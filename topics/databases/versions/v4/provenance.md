## Synthesis

- MEASURED FOR THIS CUT (v4). Three sections now rest on experiments run on 2026-07-24 rather than on published sources alone. The full harness — driver scripts, immutable raw logs, environment records, and per-lab fact-notes — is published beside this article at `topics/databases/evidence/`. Every measured figure in the article traces to a named raw file there. All labs ran PostgreSQL 16.14 (`postgres:16-alpine`, image sha256:e013e867e712) in Docker on an arm64 Mac laptop; each lab's environment.txt records the full image digest as printed by docker image inspect.
- Measured — replication (`evidence/r4-replication/`): read-your-own-writes and monotonic-read anomalies reproduced against one primary and two real streaming standbys, with lag induced deterministically by pg_wal_replay_pause() rather than by load; a lagging standby shown holding the bytes on disk unapplied (received LSN equal to the primary's sent_lsn, replay_lsn 480 bytes behind); and the per-commit cost of all five synchronous_commit settings over fifteen pgbench runs.
- Measured — replication, the finding: the synchronous_commit ladder separates at two rungs, not four. off→local and remote_write→on each separate cleanly; local→remote_write and on→remote_apply overlap entirely. The interpretation carried in the article — that the costs which appeared are two disk flushes — is labelled as interpretation, and the article states that this topology has no real network between primary and standby and therefore cannot price the network round trip.
- Measured — partitioning (`evidence/r4-partitioning/`): partition pruning shown as a verbatim EXPLAIN plan naming one partition of twelve, against a Parallel Append over all twelve when the predicate omits the partition key; DETACH PARTITION against DELETE on identical row counts (1.884–2.312 ms against 104.757–124.934 ms) with the space aftermath of each; and a time-ordered stream landing entirely in one range partition against an even spread under hash.
- Measured — partitioning, the finding that reversed a first reading: an unvacuumed unpartitioned twin flattered partitioning by roughly 119× on buffers via Heap Fetches: 203825. After vacuuming both sides the unpartitioned table won the pruned one-month count (564 buffers against 1,699). This is reported as one query shape on one shape of data, and it supports rather than undermines the section's standing verdict that partitioning is the last rung of a ladder rather than a read optimisation.
- Measured — transactions (`evidence/t1-isolation-rerun/`): a lost update at Read Committed using deliberately unequal withdrawals so the wrong result cannot be mistaken for a correct one; write skew committing at Repeatable Read and aborting at Serializable; and the SQLSTATE 40001 serialization failure captured verbatim with VERBOSITY verbose. Scope stated in the lab's own notes: this topic is saturated by the Postgres documentation and Hermitage, so the lab contributes the artifact, not a finding.
- Claim withdrawn at v4: the previous text's "a healthy follower typically under a second behind" is removed. It is a claim about production systems in the world; every lag figure now in the article was induced deliberately in a laptop container, and the two must not sit beside each other unlabelled. The claim can return with its own citation.
- Claims explicitly NOT measured and still source-derived: MySQL InnoDB's and Oracle's isolation behaviour; failover, promotion, and split brain; cross-partition transactions and two-phase commit; global versus local secondary indexes; every claim about Cassandra, Spanner, Aurora DSQL, and CockroachDB; and all LSM-tree engine behaviour. The article marks these in its own text.
- The stance itself, carried unchanged from v1: start on a general-purpose relational database, which in 2026 means Postgres, and leave it only when a measured access pattern forces the move; specialised engines are escape hatches, not starting points.
- A database is a data structure you rent over a network: every engine is a storage layout, an index structure, and a query surface fixed for one family of access patterns.
- Access-patterns-first selection: the measured workload is the only durable selection criterion; the data's conceptual shape, anticipated scale, and fashion all mislead.
- The worked examples that remain illustrative constructions rather than case studies: the social-feed fan-out, the celebrity hot key, and the engine-family comparison table.
- Index economics: every index is a redundant ordered structure that accelerates some read and taxes every write, which is why indexes are purchases rather than free lunches.
- ACID's consistency is the application's property, riding on atomicity, isolation, and durability; B-tree writes being two writes (WAL plus page) and Bloom filters keeping LSM misses cheap are standard storage-engine mechanics stated from field knowledge.
- The convergence reading: feature sets converge through multi-model creep, Postgres extensions and core work, HTAP engines, and disaggregated storage, while storage-layer physics (row versus column, B-tree versus LSM, memory versus disk, synchronous versus asynchronous) do not, so the underlying trade-offs outlive the feature checklists.
- The operational-bill argument: the true cost of a second engine is its backup regime, upgrade cadence, on-call rotation, and cross-store consistency, not its licence or feature gaps.
- The named breaking points of the Postgres-first stance: genuine global write scale, sub-millisecond p99 cache workloads, and billion-scale heavily filtered vector recall.
- Wide-column stores (Cassandra) and columnar analytics engines (ClickHouse) share a name but solve opposite problems; conflating them is a recurring selection error.
- pgvector serves corpora into the tens of millions of vectors; dedicated vector engines earn their keep at hundreds of millions, heavy filtering, or strict tenant isolation.
- Aurora DSQL and CockroachDB as the GA distributed-Postgres line, carried from the 2026-07-14 research run.
